Anderson Impurity Model

The Anderson Impurity Model is a cornerstone of condensed matter physics. It was originally designed to explain why certain non-magnetic metals (like copper) suddenly exhibit magnetic properties when small amounts of “impurities” (like iron) are added.

What do you mean “suddenly”?

Not literally instant in time 🙂. “Suddenly” here refers to a sharp change in behavior once certain physical conditions are crossed.

When a small amount of Iron is added to a non-magnetic metal like Copper, the impurity electrons can either:

  • spread out and behave like the host electrons (no magnetism), or
  • localize and form a magnetic moment

As parameters like interaction strength and hybridization are tuned, the system quickly shifts from one regime to the other. This crossover can be so sharp that magnetism appears to “turn on” all at once.

At low temperatures, this local moment can further lead to rich phenomena such as the Kondo Effect.

In short: “suddenly” = Emergence of a stable local moment once correlations dominate over hybridization.

It describes a single localized state (like an electron in a d or f orbital of a heavy metal atom) sitting inside a “sea” of free-moving conduction electrons (in other words, a localized magnetic impurity embedded in a metallic host). The model captures the essential physics of how the impurity interacts with the conduction electrons, leading to phenomena such as the Kondo effect.

The Single-Impurity Anderson Model (SIAM) Hamiltonian can be written as:

H = \sum_{\mathbf{k},\sigma} \epsilon_{\mathbf{k}}n_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} + \sum_{\mathbf{k},\sigma} [V(\mathbf{k})c_{\mathbf{k}\sigma}^{\dagger}f_{\sigma} + V^{*}(\mathbf{k})f_{\sigma}^{\dagger}c_{\mathbf{k}\sigma}] + E_f n_f + Un_{f\uparrow}n_{f\downarrow}

The Conduction Band (The “Sea”): \quad \sum_{\mathbf{k},\sigma} \epsilon_{\mathbf{k}}n_{\mathbf{k}\sigma}

This term represents the energy of the “normal” electrons in the host metal with \epsilon_{\mathbf{k}} being the kinetic energy of an electron with momentum \mathbf{k} and spin \sigma, and n_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} being the number operator (counting how many electrons are in that state).

The Hybridization / Resonance (The “Bridge”): \quad \sum_{\mathbf{k},\sigma} [V(\mathbf{k})c_{\mathbf{k}\sigma}^{\dagger}f_{\sigma} + V^{*}(\mathbf{k})f_{\sigma}^{\dagger}c_{\mathbf{k}\sigma}]

This allows electrons to hop back and forth between the metal’s conduction band (c) and the impurity atom (f).

V(\mathbf{k}) is the “mixing” strength. Without this term, the impurity and the metal would never interact. This “resonance” is what can lead to the Kondo Effect, where the impurity’s magnetic moment is eventually “screened” or hidden by the surrounding electrons at very low temperatures.

The Impurity Level

E_f n_f is the baseline energy of an electron sitting on the impurity atom. If E_f is much lower than the metal’s energy levels, the impurity will want to stay occupied.

The Coulomb Repulsion (The “Crowding” Penalty)

Un_{f\uparrow}n_{f\downarrow} is the most critical part of the model. U represents the massive energy penalty (Coulomb repulsion) that occurs if you try to put two electrons (one spin up \uparrow, one spin down \downarrow) onto the same small impurity orbital.

This is why the model is “many-body.” Because of U, the behavior of one electron depends entirely on whether another one is already there. If U is large, it prevents double occupancy, which helps the atom maintain a net magnetic spin.

The Anderson Model is the parent of many modern concepts in physics:

  • The Kondo Effect: Explaining why resistance in metals increases at near-absolute zero.
  • Heavy Fermions: Explaining materials where electrons behave as if they are 1,000 times heavier than normal.
  • Mott Insulators: Helping us understand materials that should conduct electricity but don’t because the U term (repulsion) is so strong that electrons get stuck.

The U \rightarrow \infty Limit and the Kondo Model

When U (the Coulomb repulsion) becomes very large—specifically much larger than the hybridization V—it becomes energetically “forbidden” for two electrons to occupy the impurity site simultaneously.

This leads to a famous transformation known as the Schrieffer-Wolff Transformation. In this limit, the Anderson model simplifies into the Kondo Model.

The Schrieffer–Wolff transformation is a unitary transformation used to determine an effective (often low-energy) Hamiltonian by decoupling weakly interacting subspaces

When U is massive, the impurity site is restricted to having exactly one electron (it stays “singly occupied”). Because the electron can’t be joined by another, it can only interact with the surrounding “sea” by swapping its spin.

  1. Virtual Hopping: An electron from the conduction band can “try” to hop onto the impurity site for a tiny fraction of a second.
  2. Exchange Interaction: Because U is so high, it immediately hops back off. However, during this “virtual” process, the spin of the impurity electron and the conduction electron can flip.
  3. The Result: The Hamiltonian simplifies from a model of moving particles to a model of interacting spins.

The Kondo Hamiltonian

In this case the complex equation you saw earlier collapses into a much simpler “Exchange” form:

H_{Kondo} = \sum_{\mathbf{k},\sigma} \epsilon_{\mathbf{k}}n_{\mathbf{k}\sigma} + J \mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{s}(0)

  • J (Exchange Coupling): This represents the strength of the interaction. In the Anderson model, J is derived from the other variables (J \approx \frac{V^2}{U}).
  • \mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{s}(0): This is a “Spin-Spin” interaction. It says the energy of the system depends on whether the impurity spin (\mathbf{S}) and the nearby metal electron spin (\mathbf{s}) are pointing in the same or opposite directions.

Why is this significant? Because it captures the essence of the Kondo effect: in this limit, at very low temperatures, the conduction electrons “screen” the impurity. They form a cloud around the iron atom with opposite spins, effectively making the magnetic moment disappear. This is why the electrical resistance of some metals actually increases as you cool them down—the electrons are getting “stuck” or scattered by this cloud.
