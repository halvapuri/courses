Weyl Semi-Metals

How do Weyl Semi-Metals emerge? We shall see that central to this is the role of SOC in causing the conduction and valence bands to invert.

Start from atomic orbitals…

Imagine a crystal made of atoms contributing s-like and p-like orbitals. At the \Gamma-point (k=0), the bands can be labeled by their parity eigenvalues under inversion symmetry \mathcal{P}. Let’s call:

  • |s\rangle: even parity (+)
  • |p\rangle: odd parity (-)

The unperturbed energies are:

E_s > E_p.

So normally the s-like band becomes the conduction band and p-like the valence band.

More mathematically using the k\cdot p expansion:

E_n(\mathbf{k}) = E_n(0) + \frac{\hbar^2 k^2}{2m^*} + \dots

Thus the ordering at k=0 sets the global band topology but if SOC pushes E_{p}(0) above E_{s}(0), the bands are inverted at \Gamma.

Away from \Gamma, the k^2-term (Bk^2 in the BHZ model) restores normal ordering, so the band gap opens up again — but the parity order at \Gamma stays inverted, and that’s what counts topologically.

  • \Gamma = (0,0,0), the center of the Brillouin zone, is a time-reversal invariant momentum (TRIM). \Gamma \equiv -\Gamma \; (\text{mod reciprocal lattice vector}).
  • There are only a few TRIMs: \Gamma, X, L, M, etc., depending on the lattice. These are high-symmetry points where crystal momentum is zero (why?), so Bloch states have the same symmetry as the atomic orbitals.
  • Each state can be labeled by parity eigenvalues and other crystal quantum numbers.

This is crucial for topology.

Why does inversion occure there?

Well in local terms… - Near \Gamma, the band energies are close to the atomic orbital levels because k is small, meaning there is no kinetic energy term yet.
- This is where the s- and p-like states from atoms interact and hybridize. - In fact topological invariants are defined at TRIMs. Fu & Kane showed the \mathbb{Z}_2 topological invariant can be computed as a product of parity eigenvalues at TRIMs:

(-1)^\nu = \prod_{i\in \text{TRIM}} \prod_{n\in\text{occ}} \xi_n(\Gamma_i),

where \xi_n=\pm1 are parity eigenvalues.

  • This only works at TRIMs because time-reversal forces Kramers degeneracy there.
  • So band inversion must occur at a TRIM (like \Gamma) to flip the parity product and change the topological invariant. It’s the natural place where atomic s- and p-states meet.
  • Only inversion at a TRIM like \Gamma can change the topological invariant. If inversion happened at some generic \mathbf{k}-point, it would happen at \mathbf{k} and -\mathbf{k} simultaneously (due to time-reversal symmetry), and their contributions would cancel — leaving the \mathbb{Z}_2 invariant unchanged. In other words only inversions at TRIMs can drive topological phase transitions.
Why \Gamma point matters Explanation
High symmetry Wavefunctions have well-defined parity at k=0.
SOC is strongest here Small k: atomic character dominates.
TRIM condition Needed for parity-based topological invariant.
Topology changes here first Inversion here forces gap closure → topological transition.

Parity eigenvalues are only well-defined at TRIM points such as \Gamma, because only there does inversion act within the same Bloch subspace. Equivalently, only at TRIMs are the Bloch States also parity eigenstates. Take a Bloch state:

\psi_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}) = e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}\,u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}), \qquad u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}+\mathbf{R})=u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}),

where u_{n\mathbf{k}} has the periodicity of the lattice and applying inversion you get:

\hat{P}\,\psi_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}) = \psi_{n\mathbf{k}}(-\mathbf{r}) = e^{-i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}\,u_{n\mathbf{k}}(-\mathbf{r}) \;\propto\; \psi_{n,-\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}).

Thus inversion maps \mathbf{k}\to -\mathbf{k}. Only when \mathbf{k}=-\mathbf{k} (mod a reciprocal lattice vector) does \hat{P} act within the same subspace. These special points are the time-reversal invariant momenta (TRIMs), including \Gamma = (0,0,0). At TRIM points, each Bloch state can be chosen as an eigenstate of inversion:

\hat{P}\,\psi_{n\mathbf{k}} = \xi_n(\mathbf{k})\,\psi_{n\mathbf{k}}, \qquad \xi_n(\mathbf{k})=\pm1.

But why are \mathbf{k} and -\mathbf{k} different subspaces?

Because Bloch states are eigenstates of lattice translations T_\mathbf{R}:

T_\mathbf{R}\psi_{n\mathbf{k}} = e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{R}} \psi_{n\mathbf{k}}.

States with different \mathbf{k} have different translation eigenvalues and thus belong to orthogonal invariant subspaces.

For \mathbf{k}\neq -\mathbf{k}:

T_\mathbf{R}\psi_{n\mathbf{k}} = e^{+i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{R}}\psi_{n\mathbf{k}},\qquad T_\mathbf{R}\psi_{n,-\mathbf{k}} = e^{-i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{R}}\psi_{n,-\mathbf{k}}.

These are inequivalent irreducible representations of the translation group. Therefore, \hat{P} maps between two different subspaces unless \mathbf{k}=-\mathbf{k}.

To summarize:

\mathbf{k} point Action of \hat{P} Parity eigenvalues possible?
Generic \mathbf{k} \psi_{\mathbf{k}} \mapsto \psi_{-\mathbf{k}} (different subspace) ❌ No
TRIM (\mathbf{k}=-\mathbf{k}) \psi_{\mathbf{k}} \mapsto \pm\psi_{\mathbf{k}} (same subspace) ✅ Yes

and include spin–orbit coupling (SOC)…

The atomic SOC Hamiltonian is

H_\text{SOC} = \lambda \,\mathbf{L}\cdot\mathbf{S},

which splits the p-states into:

|p_{3/2}\rangle \; \text{(4-fold)}, \qquad |p_{1/2}\rangle \; \text{(2-fold)}.

If SOC is weak, the ordering stays the same: E_s > E_{p_{3/2}}>E_{p_{1/2}}.
If SOC is strong, it can push the p_{1/2}-like state above the s-like state, inverting the order.

effecting band inversion seen via the k\cdot p Hamiltonian: BHZ model

The simplest way to see inversion mathematically is the Bernevig–Hughes–Zhang (BHZ) model for HgTe/CdTe:

H(\mathbf{k}) = \begin{pmatrix} M(\mathbf{k}) & A k_+ & 0 & 0 \\ A k_- & -M(\mathbf{k}) & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & M(\mathbf{k}) & -A k_- \\ 0 & 0 & -A k_+ & -M(\mathbf{k}) \end{pmatrix},

where

M(\mathbf{k}) = M_0 - B k^2,\qquad k_\pm = k_x \pm i k_y.

  • The sign of M_0 determines the ordering at k=0:
    • M_0>0: normal (s-above-p)
    • M_0<0: inverted (p-above-s)

Band energies:

E(\mathbf{k}) = \pm \sqrt{M(\mathbf{k})^2 + A^2 k^2}.

This topological transition and invariant…

When M_0 changes sign:

  • The gap closes at \mathbf{k}=0 when M_0=0.
  • The parity eigenvalues at time-reversal invariant momenta (TRIMs) change.
  • This changes the \mathbb{Z}_2 topological invariant (Fu–Kane formula):

(-1)^\nu = \prod_{i=1}^{8} \prod_{n\in\text{occ}} \xi_{2n}(\Gamma_i),

where \xi=\pm1 are the parity eigenvalues of the occupied bands at each TRIM \Gamma_i.

  • Normal insulator: \nu = 0
  • Band-inverted: \nu = 1 → topological insulator

This is the rigorous meaning of:
> “You cannot undo inversion without closing the gap somewhere.”

is how Dirac and Weyl semimetals appear

Now suppose you tune M_0 to exactly zero:

M(\mathbf{k}) = - Bk^2.

Then the bands touch at \mathbf{k}=0:

E(\mathbf{k}) = \pm A|\mathbf{k}|.

This is a Dirac point: a 4-fold degeneracy (2 spin × 2 orbital).
This gives a Dirac semimetal (DSM) phase.

  • Dirac point is protected if both inversion (\mathcal{P}) and time-reversal (\mathcal{T}) symmetries are present and band crossings are symmetry-protected.

Break \mathcal{P} or \mathcal{T} → Weyl semimetal

If you break either inversion or time-reversal, the Dirac point splits into two Weyl points of opposite chirality.

Mathematically:

  • Add a term like b\,\sigma_z (breaking inversion) or b\,s_z (breaking time-reversal) to the Dirac Hamiltonian:

H_\text{Dirac} = v\mathbf{k}\cdot \boldsymbol{\Gamma} + b\Gamma_5.

→ This shifts the Weyl nodes to \pm \mathbf{k}_0 in the Brillouin zone.

  • Each Weyl node is a Berry curvature monopole with Chern number \pm1.
  • This gives Weyl semimetal (WSM) with Fermi arc surface states.

Phase diagram summary

M_0 value SOC gap? Symmetries Phase Key feature
M_0>0 yes \mathcal{P},\mathcal{T} Normal insulator trivial gap
M_0<0 yes \mathcal{P},\mathcal{T} Topological insulator protected edge/surface states
M_0=0 gap closes \mathcal{P},\mathcal{T} Dirac semimetal (DSM) Dirac point
M_0=0, break \mathcal{P} or \mathcal{T} gapless broken \mathcal{P} or \mathcal{T} Weyl semimetal (WSM) Weyl points + Fermi arcs

Summary

  • Band inversion flips orbital/parity order → changes topological invariant.
  • If SOC gaps the crossing → topological insulator.
  • If gap doesn’t open → Dirac semimetal.
  • If inversion/time-reversal is broken → Dirac point splits → Weyl semimetal.

This entire sequence is a single continuous picture governed by the sign of the mass term M_0 and the presence of symmetries.

Bloch states at \mathbf{k} and -\mathbf{k} are linked by time-reversal. This also means that TRS complex conjugates the wavefunction.

At TRIMs, they become Kramers pairs with guaranteed degeneracy.

Why Kramers degeneracy at TRIMs matters

The time-reversal operator is anti-unitary: \Theta = U_T \mathcal{K}, where \mathcal{K} is complex conjugation and U_T = e^{-i\pi S_y/\hbar} for spin-\frac{1}{2} particles. For spin-\frac{1}{2}, it satisfies \Theta^2 = -1.

At TRIM, Kramers theorem then guarantees that each state |u_n(\mathbf{k})\rangle has a degenerate partner \Theta|u_n(\mathbf{k})\rangle with the same energy.

Because \Theta also flips momentum: \Theta|u_n(\mathbf{k})\rangle = |u_n(-\mathbf{k})\rangle, the degeneracy occurs only when \mathbf{k} = -\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{G}

(where \mathbf{G} is a reciprocal lattice vector), which defines the time-reversal invariant momenta (TRIMs).

At TRIMs:

  • Every band forms a Kramers pair
  • Band inversions occur as pairs, allowing well-defined parity eigenvalues
  • The \mathbb{Z}_2 topological invariant can be computed purely from the parities of the occupied bands at the TRIMs (Fu–Kane formula)

Away from TRIMs, such degeneracy is not guaranteed, so the topology cannot be determined locally there.
TRIMs act as topological anchors for time-reversal invariant systems.

❖ Do we want time-reversal symmetry?

Whether TRS is desired depends on the target topological phase:

Phase TRS required? Reason
Topological insulator (\mathbb{Z}_2) Yes TRS enforces Kramers degeneracy and protects edge states
Weyl semimetal No (must break TRS or inversion) TRS would pair Weyl nodes at \mathbf{k} and -\mathbf{k}, destroying them
Chern insulator No (must break TRS) TRS forces Berry curvature \Omega(\mathbf{k}) to cancel: \Omega(\mathbf{k}) = -\Omega(-\mathbf{k}), giving zero Chern number
