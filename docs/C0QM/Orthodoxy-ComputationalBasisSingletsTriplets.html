Computational vs. Singlet/Triplet Basis for Two-Electron Systems

See also

In many-body quantum mechanics, we often describe electrons using a computational basis, which simply labels the spin state of each electron. For a spin-\frac{1}{2} system, the basic single-electron states are

|u\rangle = |\uparrow\rangle, \quad |d\rangle = |\downarrow\rangle

and a two-electron system can be written as

|uu\rangle, \quad |ud\rangle, \quad |du\rangle, \quad |dd\rangle.

This computational basis is extremely useful in numerical methods such as exact diagonalization, tensor network methods, and quantum computing. Each basis state corresponds to a binary occupation of spin-orbitals, making it natural to represent the Hilbert space as bitstrings like |01\rangle or |10\rangle. Operators such as creation and annihilation operators act directly on this basis, and the antisymmetry of electrons is automatically enforced via the fermionic commutation relations:

\{ c_i^\dagger, c_j^\dagger \} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad c_i^\dagger c_j^\dagger |0\rangle = - c_j^\dagger c_i^\dagger |0\rangle.

Thus, while a state like |ud\rangle appears “unsymmetrized,” the correct fermionic antisymmetry is encoded in the algebra of the operators.

However, sometimes it is advantageous to switch to the singlet/triplet basis, which explicitly encodes the symmetry of the spin wavefunction. For two electrons, this basis is defined as:

|S=0, M=0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|ud\rangle - |du\rangle),

|S=1, M=1\rangle = |uu\rangle, \quad |S=1, M=0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} (|ud\rangle + |du\rangle), \quad |S=1, M=-1\rangle = |dd\rangle.

The singlet is antisymmetric under particle exchange, while the triplet states are symmetric.

Example

The singlet/triplet basis is particularly useful when the Hamiltonian of the system respects spin symmetry, such as the Heisenberg exchange Hamiltonian:

H = J \vec{S}_1 \cdot \vec{S}_2,

where \vec{S}_i is the spin operator of electron i. In the computational basis, the Hamiltonian mixes the states |ud\rangle and |du\rangle, and one must diagonalize the 2\times 2 subspace to find the eigenstates.

\vec{S}_1 \cdot \vec{S}_2 |ud\rangle = \frac{1}{2}(|du\rangle - |ud\rangle) (plus\ diagonal\ term)

In contrast, the singlet/triplet basis diagonalizes the Hamiltonian automatically, since these states are eigenstates of total spin S^2:

  • Singlet: S=0, eigenvalue S(S+1) = 0
  • Triplet: S=1, eigenvalue S(S+1) = 2

By working in the singlet/triplet basis, the symmetry of the problem is made explicit, and the relationship between spin and orbital symmetry becomes transparent. For example, a singlet spin state requires a symmetric spatial (orbital) wavefunction to satisfy overall fermionic antisymmetry, while a triplet spin state requires an antisymmetric spatial wavefunction.

  • The computational basis is ideal for bookkeeping, numerical representation, and operator algebra. Antisymmetry is enforced automatically by the operators.
  • The singlet/triplet basis is ideal for exploiting spin symmetry, diagonalizing spin Hamiltonians like the Heisenberg Hamiltonian, and understanding the connection between spin and spatial symmetry.
