Fermionic Systems and Hamiltonians

Fermionic Hamiltonians possess structural features that arise directly from the algebra satisfied by their creation and annihilation operators. These operators obey the canonical anticommutation relations

\{c_i, c_j^\dagger\} = \delta_{ij}, \qquad \{c_i, c_j\} = 0 .

This algebra implies several immediate consequences. First, fermionic creation operators satisfy

(c_i^\dagger)^2 = 0 ,

which expresses the Pauli exclusion principle: a single quantum state can be occupied by at most one fermion. The number operator

n_i = c_i^\dagger c_i

therefore has eigenvalues

n_i \in \{0,1\}.

Each single-particle orbital behaves as a two-level system, corresponding to the presence or absence of a fermion. This simple structure already distinguishes fermionic systems sharply from bosonic ones, whose occupation numbers can take arbitrarily large values.

A generic fermionic Hamiltonian written in second quantization takes the form

H = \sum_{ij} t_{ij} c_i^\dagger c_j + \sum_{ijkl} V_{ijkl} c_i^\dagger c_j^\dagger c_k c_l .

The first term typically describes single-particle motion such as hopping on a lattice, while the second describes interactions between particles. A notable feature of such Hamiltonians is that every term contains an even number of fermionic operators. This property is not accidental: it is tied to a fundamental symmetry known as fermion parity.

Define the total particle number operator

N = \sum_i c_i^\dagger c_i .

But many physical Hamiltonians do not conserve N. A famous example is superconductivity (BCS theory), where Cooper pairs can be created or destroyed. However, something does remain conserved: enter the fermion parity operator

P = (-1)^N .

States with an even number of fermions have eigenvalue (+1), while states with an odd number have eigenvalue (-1). Because typical fermionic Hamiltonians contain only even numbers of creation and annihilation operators, one finds

[H, (-1)^N] = 0 .

Thus fermion parity is conserved. The Hilbert space separates into two sectors—states with even particle number and states with odd particle number—and the Hamiltonian never mixes them.

Fermion Parity and the Meaning of Parity

The word parity can lead to confusion. In relativistic quantum mechanics, parity typically refers to a spatial symmetry, while in many-body fermionic systems it refers to the evenness or oddness of the total particle number. Although these meanings are different physically, they share a common mathematical structure: both are \mathbb{Z}_2 symmetries whose operators square to the identity.

Spatial Parity

In quantum mechanics, spatial parity corresponds to inversion of spatial coordinates \mathbf{x} \rightarrow -\mathbf{x}.

The corresponding operator P acts on wavefunctions as (P\psi)(\mathbf{x}) = \psi(-\mathbf{x}).

Applying the transformation twice restores the original state, P^2 = 1.

Because of this property, energy eigenstates can be chosen as eigenstates of parity with eigenvalues P = \pm 1.

States with eigenvalue +1 are called even under parity, while those with eigenvalue -1 are odd. For example, \psi(x) = \cos x \quad \text{is even}, \qquad \psi(x) = \sin x \quad \text{is odd}.

Thus spatial parity distinguishes whether a wavefunction is symmetric or antisymmetric under reflection.

Fermion Parity

In many-body fermionic systems, the term refers instead to the parity of the total fermion number. Define the particle number operator N = \sum_i c_i^\dagger c_i, where c_i^\dagger and c_i are fermionic creation and annihilation operators.

The fermion parity operator is defined as P = (-1)^N.

Its action on a Fock state |n\rangle with n fermions is P |n\rangle = (-1)^n |n\rangle.

Thus \text{even number of fermions} \Rightarrow P = +1, \text{odd number of fermions} \Rightarrow P = -1.

In other words, the operator P measures whether the total fermion number is even or odd. The terminology ``parity’’ here reflects the mathematical meaning of parity as the classification of integers into even and odd.

Transformation of Fermionic Operators

Fermionic annihilation operators change the particle number by one, c_j |n\rangle \rightarrow |n-1\rangle.

Conjugating a fermionic operator with the parity operator gives P c_j P^{-1} = -c_j.

Thus fermionic operators are odd under fermion parity. By contrast, operators containing an even number of fermionic fields, such as c_i^\dagger c_j, are invariant under parity.

Because physical observables must commute with the parity operator, [P,O] = 0,

only operators containing an even number of fermionic operators can represent measurable quantities. This leads to the fermion parity superselection rule discussed in many-body theory.

Parity as a Subgroup of Particle-Number Symmetry

The parity operator can also be written in exponential form P = e^{i\pi N}.

This expression arises from the continuous U(1) symmetry associated with particle-number conservation, U(\theta) = e^{i\theta N}.

Under this transformation the fermionic operators transform as U(\theta) c_j U^\dagger(\theta) = e^{-i\theta} c_j.

Choosing the special value \theta = \pi gives P = e^{i\pi N}, which produces the transformation c_j \rightarrow -c_j.

Thus fermion parity may be understood as the \mathbb{Z}_2 subgroup of the global U(1) particle-number symmetry.

So although spatial parity and fermion parity refer to different physical transformations, both share the same mathematical structure as \mathbb{Z}_2 symmetries. Spatial parity distinguishes wavefunctions that are even or odd under spatial reflection, while fermion parity distinguishes states containing an even or odd number of fermions. The latter plays a central role in many-body physics because physical observables must preserve fermion parity, leading to the fermion parity superselection rule.

At first sight this might appear to be merely a feature of particular Hamiltonians. In fact it reflects a deeper principle. Observable operators must behave like bosonic operators: they must commute with other observables at spacelike separation in order to preserve locality and causality. A single fermionic operator does not satisfy this requirement because fermionic fields anticommute. As a result, physical observables must always contain an even number of fermionic fields, ensuring that they commute at large separations.

This restriction leads to what is known as the fermion parity superselection rule. Because physical observables commute with the parity operator (P), states belonging to different parity sectors cannot be connected by any observable operation. In practice this means that coherent superpositions of states with even and odd fermion number cannot be observed. The Hilbert space therefore decomposes into two superselection sectors corresponding to even and odd fermion parity.

These ideas become particularly clear when one examines how fermionic operators are represented in many-body systems. In certain constructions—such as the Jordan–Wigner transformation used to map fermions onto spin systems—the annihilation operator for mode (j) contains a phase factor of the form

(-1)^{\sum_{k=1}^{j-1} N_k},

where

N_k = c_k^\dagger c_k

counts the occupation of orbitals preceding (j) in some chosen ordering. This phase ensures that the fermionic operators obey the correct anticommutation relations. However, it introduces an interesting subtlety: the phase depends on the occupation numbers of other modes. Since the ordering of modes is merely a matter of convention, physical predictions must ultimately be independent of such choices.

A deeper concern arises if one imagines measuring an operator containing an odd number of fermionic creation or annihilation operators. Because of the phase string above, the result of such a measurement could depend on the occupation numbers of distant modes. One could then imagine a situation in which one fermionic mode is located far away—for instance on Mars—while other modes are measured on Earth. The phase appearing in the measurement outcome could reveal whether a particle is present on Mars or not. In principle this would allow instantaneous communication across arbitrary distances, violating the principle of causality.

The resolution of this apparent paradox is precisely the fermion parity superselection rule. Operators containing an odd number of fermionic operators are not physical observables. Only operators that commute with the parity operator are allowed. Such operators always contain an even number of fermionic creation and annihilation operators. When written out explicitly, the phase strings associated with the Jordan–Wigner representation cancel in pairs, ensuring that the resulting observable is local and independent of the ordering of modes.

Another remarkable symmetry that appears naturally in fermionic systems is particle–hole symmetry. Because each orbital can only be either empty or occupied, the absence of a particle may be viewed as a distinct quasiparticle known as a hole. Mathematically this idea can be expressed through the transformation

c_i \rightarrow c_i^\dagger .

Under this transformation the number operator changes as

n_i = c_i^\dagger c_i \;\rightarrow\; 1 - n_i .

An occupied state becomes empty, and an empty state becomes occupied. Because fermionic occupation numbers take only the values (0) or (1), this transformation maps the Hilbert space onto itself in a natural way.

Particle–hole symmetry frequently appears in lattice models. Consider the tight-binding Hamiltonian

H = -t \sum_{\langle ij\rangle} c_i^\dagger c_j .

On a bipartite lattice one may define the transformation

c_i \rightarrow (-1)^i c_i^\dagger .

Using the anticommutation relation

c_i c_j^\dagger = -c_j^\dagger c_i + \delta_{ij},

one finds that the Hamiltonian transforms back into itself up to constant energy shifts. The energy spectrum then becomes symmetric around half filling: particle excitations above the Fermi level correspond directly to hole excitations below it.

Particle–hole symmetry becomes especially important in superconductivity. In the Bogoliubov–de Gennes formalism, particle and hole degrees of freedom are combined into the Nambu spinor

\Psi = \begin{pmatrix} c \\ c^\dagger \end{pmatrix}.

The Bogoliubov–de Gennes Hamiltonian satisfies a symmetry relation

C H(k) C^{-1} = -H(-k),

where (C) is the particle–hole operator. This symmetry ensures that every positive-energy quasiparticle state has a corresponding negative-energy partner.

A particularly illuminating way to see the structure of fermionic systems is to rewrite fermionic operators in terms of Majorana operators

\gamma_1 = c + c^\dagger,

\gamma_2 = i(c^\dagger - c).

These operators are Hermitian and satisfy the algebra

\{\gamma_i, \gamma_j\} = 2\delta_{ij}.

Each complex fermion can therefore be viewed as being composed of two real Majorana modes. In this representation the fermion parity operator for a single mode takes the simple form

P = i\,\gamma_1 \gamma_2 .

Physical observables must contain even numbers of Majorana operators, once again reflecting the underlying parity constraint. This perspective is particularly useful in the study of topological superconductors, where spatially separated Majorana modes can encode quantum information in the global fermion parity of the system.

Taken together, these features illustrate how deeply the algebra of fermionic operators shapes the structure of many-body physics. Anticommutation relations lead to the Pauli exclusion principle, the emergence of fermion parity conservation and its associated superselection rule, the natural appearance of particle–hole symmetry, and the Majorana representation that plays an important role in modern condensed matter theory.
