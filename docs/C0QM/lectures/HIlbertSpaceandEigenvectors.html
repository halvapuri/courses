By Way of Analogy

We have been discussing how the best way to understand a subject is to cast it into familiar terms; in situations that everyone can understand.

Take this equation for instance. It is commonly encountered in a course in quantum mechanics:

|X_{n\mathbf Q}\rangle = \sum_{v c \mathbf k} A^{n\mathbf Q}_{v c \mathbf k} |v c \mathbf k \mathbf Q\rangle.

What this equation is saying is […].

Good — this is exactly the right place to be picky. The short answer is: **no, (A^{nQ}_{vck}) is not a ket like (|vckQ)**. It is a column vector of numbers, while (|vckQ) is a column vector of states. Let me prove this carefully and cleanly, step by step, using linear algebra only.

Fix (\mathbf Q). Consider the excitonic Hilbert subspace \mathcal H_{\mathbf Q} = \text{span}\{|v c \mathbf k \mathbf Q\rangle\}.

Each (|v c \mathbf k \mathbf Q\rangle) is a basis vector in a Hilbert space.

But we could lump this indices together so that \alpha \equiv (v,c,\mathbf k) and Then we write the basis as |\alpha\rangle \equiv |v c \mathbf k \mathbf Q\rangle.

In linear algebra there are two different vector spaces involved. There are the (A) hysical but abstract Hilbert space vectors which live in the abstract Hilbert space, $ |H_{Q}. $ and there are the (B) coordinate vectors (column vectors of numbers). These live in (\mathbb C^N) and depend on a chosen basis:

\vec \psi = \begin{pmatrix} \psi_1 \ \psi_2 \ \vdots \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \psi_\alpha = \langle \alpha | \psi \rangle.

They are not states. They are representations of states.

A ket is not a column vector of numbers until you choose a basis.

Now we need to make sense of this statement and for that we turn to Shakespeare. By way of analogy, we can think Hamlet as a character in an abstract space. Indeed, we do not know much about Hamlet looks like. How tall or short is he? What is the exact complexion of his skin or the color of his hair.

To be able to place a face on him, we need to select an actor. Or to put another way, the

Thus we have three different realms. The realm of characters in which Hamlet lives. But Hamlet is an abstraction without flesh and blood and to be given flesh and blood we need to bind him to an actor.

We may conclude that different actors highlight different aspects of the same underlying reality. Hence Olivier’s version would be different from Burton’s.

Now Hamlet as it occupies the society’s consciousness might be a fusion of several of these interpretations.

8. Why (A^n) is NOT a ket

  • (|): element of Hilbert space (H_{Q})
  • (A^n_): complex number
  • ((A^n_)): column vector in (C^N)

They live in different vector spaces.

Calling both “vectors” is standard but dangerous.

10. Final conclusion (formal statement)

Theorem (Exciton BSE diagonalization) Given the basis (|v c k Q),

  1. The exciton Hamiltonian defines a matrix (H_{}).
  2. Its eigenvectors (A^n) are coordinate vectors.
  3. Each eigenvector uniquely defines a physical exciton state by [ |X_{nQ}= {v c k} A^{nQ}{v c k} |v c k Q.]

No contradiction, no double counting, no operator-state confusion — just representation theory.

Once again we need to lean on our ability to make stories. This time we turn to the doddering Prince of Denmark who couldn’t quite make up his mind this way or that.

Think first about a familiar situation outside physics. Hamlet is a character. He exists as an abstract role in a play. Hamlet is not tall or short, not young or old, not British or Pakistani. None of those properties belong to Hamlet himself. They belong to the actor who plays Hamlet in a particular performance.

Now imagine a theater company. For a given production, they choose a specific cast. One night Hamlet is played by Actor A, another night by Actor B. The character Hamlet is the same, but the representation of Hamlet has changed.

This distinction—between the abstract character and its concrete realization—is exactly the distinction between a Hilbert-space vector and its representation in a chosen basis.

In quantum mechanics, a physical state is an abstract object. We denote it by a ket, |\psi\rangle. This object lives in a Hilbert space. It is not a column of numbers. It does not have components until we choose a basis. Just as Hamlet does not have a height until an actor is chosen, a quantum state does not have coordinates until a basis is fixed.

Now fix the center-of-mass momentum \mathbf Q of an exciton. We define a Hilbert space \mathcal H_{\mathbf Q} spanned by the electron–hole pair states |v c \mathbf k \mathbf Q\rangle. Each of these states corresponds to “an electron in band c at \mathbf k+\mathbf Q/2 and a hole in band v at \mathbf k-\mathbf Q/2”. These states form a basis: $$ v c k Q | v’ c’ k’ Q ====================================================================

{vv’}{cc’}{kk’}, and they resolve the identity, {v c k} |v c k Q v c k Q| ================================

{H{Q}}. $$

In the analogy, these basis states are the actors available to play Hamlet.

The exciton itself is a physical state, |X_{n\mathbf Q}\rangle \in \mathcal H_{\mathbf Q}. This is Hamlet. It is not tied to any single actor.

Because the basis is complete, the state can always be expanded as $$ |X_{nQ} ======================

{v c k} |v c k Q v c k Q | X{nQ}. We now *define* the coefficients A^{nQ}{v c k} v c k Q | X{nQ}. With this definition, |X_{nQ} ======================

{v c k} A^{nQ}{v c k} |v c k Q. $$

These coefficients are just complex numbers. They tell us “how much” the exciton looks like each electron–hole pair state. In the analogy, they are the weights describing which actor is playing Hamlet and how strongly that actor contributes to the role.

Now comes the crucial distinction.

The set of numbers {A^{n\mathbf Q}_{v c \mathbf k}} can be arranged into a column, $$ A^{nQ} ======================

\begin{pmatrix} A^{n\mathbf Q}*{1} \ A^{n\mathbf Q}*{2} \ \vdots \end{pmatrix}

}*{2}
\end{pmatrix}. $$ This object lives in \mathbb C^N. It is a coordinate vector. It is not a physical state. It depends on the chosen basis {|v c \mathbf k \mathbf Q\rangle}. If we change basis, this column changes, even though the physical state |X_{n\mathbf Q}\rangle does not.

Hamlet is not the list of actors. The list of actors is only one way to represent Hamlet.

The exciton Hamiltonian H^X(\mathbf Q) is an operator acting on \mathcal H_{\mathbf Q}. The physical eigenvalue problem is $$ H^X(Q),|X_{nQ} =====================================

E^X_{nQ},|X_{nQ}. $$ This equation lives entirely in Hilbert space. No matrices have appeared yet.

To turn this into something computable, we choose the basis {|v c \mathbf k \mathbf Q\rangle}. We define matrix elements $$ H_{(v c k),(v’ c’ k’)}(Q) =================================================

v c k Q | H^X(Q) | v’ c’ k’ Q. Projecting the operator equation onto the basis gives {v’ c’ k’} H{(v c k),(v’ c’ k’)}(Q) ,A^{nQ}_{v’ c’ k’} ==================================

E^X_{nQ} ,A^{nQ}_{v c k}. This is a **matrix eigenvalue problem**: H(Q),A^{nQ} ===================================

EX_{nQ},A{nQ}. $$

Here the eigenvector is \mathbf A^{n\mathbf Q}, the column of numbers. This eigenvector lives in coordinate space, not in the Hilbert space of physical states.

Finally, once the matrix problem is solved, the physical state is reconstructed as |X_{n\mathbf Q}\rangle = \sum_{v c \mathbf k} A^{n\mathbf Q}_{v c \mathbf k} |v c \mathbf k \mathbf Q\rangle.

This step is not diagonalization; it is reconstruction. It is how the abstract character Hamlet is realized by a specific cast.

So when you ask, “If A^{n\mathbf Q}_{v c \mathbf k} is an eigenvector, why isn’t it a ket like |v c \mathbf k \mathbf Q\rangle?”, the answer is now clear:

The ket is Hamlet. The column vector is the casting list.

They are related, but they do not live in the same space, and confusing them is the source of nearly all notation headaches in many-body theory.
